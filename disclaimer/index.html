As part of an academic and non-intrusive cybersecurity research project, we are performing an automated DNS scan targeting domains within the .nl top-level domain (TLD). The primary goal of this research is to identify and map subdomain structures exposed through DNS mechanisms. A secondary objective is to evaluate the prevalence and usage of DNSSEC, including NSEC and NSEC3 implementations.

  • Type of research: DNS resolution only (no exploitation or unauthorized access)
  • Techniques: DNS queries made directly or via Google Public DNS (8.8.8.8 / 8.8.4.4)
  • Data collected: Public DNS records, including subdomains exposed via NSEC/NSEC3
  • Per-domain traffic limit: Max. 50 DNS requests per second
  • Scope: Domains under the .nl TLD only
  • Purpose: Increase awareness of subdomain exposure risks and DNSSEC-related privacy implications

1. Is this scan harmful to my systems or infrastructure?

No. Only standard DNS queries are made through Google DNS. There are no penetration attempts, port scans, or unauthorized access attempts.

2. Why use Google DNS?

We use Google Public DNS because it provides robust caching. This significantly reduces the number of duplicate queries reaching authoritative servers, minimizing load and making the scan more efficient and respectful of infrastructure.

3. Are any personal or sensitive data collected?

No. Only publicly available DNS data is collected. We do not gather or process personal data or confidential business information.

4. Will the research results be published?

Yes. Aggregated, anonymized findings will be made publicly available after the study concludes. If you're interested in being notified, feel free to get in touch via the opt-out email address.

5. What are NSEC and NSEC3, and why are they relevant?

NSEC and NSEC3 are DNSSEC mechanisms that help prove a subdomain does not exist. However, depending on implementation, they may inadvertently reveal existing subdomains (zone walking), which can present privacy and security concerns.
